Aki Taha writes a newsletter I've followed for years. A while back he published a piece called *The Easy Bus Never Comes Around* — a meditation on the value of hard work, hard things, and not ever expecting “easy” to come around.

But I want to offer a complication. Because I've also lived the other side of it — and I think it's a trap worth naming.

The easy bus does come. And when it does, you might not notice you've been riding it too long.

---

I spent eight years at a language school in Singapore. I came in terrified, teaching over 30 hours a week and spending another hour or two each night preparing for the next day's classes — a full day's work, every day, with no room to coast. I obsessed over lesson plans. I was recognised, rewarded, and growing. The early years were hard in all the right ways. I was building something. Every difficult lesson was a lesson. Every uncomfortable conversation with a student made me better. I was handling hard well.

And then — slowly, quietly — things got easier.

Not because I stopped caring. Because I got good. I knew the curriculum cold. I knew how to read a classroom. I had rhythms that worked. The hard thing had become the comfortable thing, and that felt like progress.

For a while, it was.

---

Here's what nobody talks about in the *handle hard better* conversation: mastery eventually produces comfort, and comfort isn't always the enemy. When ease is the product of genuine competence, it gives you room to go deeper, to mentor others, to build something that outlasts you. That kind of earned ease is worth protecting.

But there's another kind of comfort. The kind that sneaks in not because you've mastered something, but because you've stopped asking whether the thing still deserves your best.

The rhythms that were once discipline become default. The job that once stretched you now fits perfectly. And perfect fit, held too long, starts to feel like growth when it's actually just the absence of friction.

The cost isn't visible in the moment. It accumulates quietly. You don't suffer — you just plateau. And plateaus, unlike hard things, don't announce themselves.

---

I look back at my ULC years and I'm proud of most of them. The community we built. The students we helped. The teachers I worked alongside. Real things.

But I also know that in the last few years, I was on the easy bus. The salary hadn't grown. The role hadn't evolved. The challenges I was solving were the same ones I'd been solving for years. I wasn't handling hard — I was handling familiar.

The warning signs were there. I just had to convince myself they were stability.

---

So here's what I'd add to Aki's framing, not to replace it but to complete it:

The easy bus does come. It arrives after years of hard work, and you've earned your seat. Get on.

But keep asking: *am I still moving?*

If the ease is giving you room to go deeper — to mentor, to build, to serve at a higher level — stay. You've earned the comfort. Use it.

If the ease has quietly become the destination — if the scenery stopped changing and you stopped noticing — that's not stability. That's the bus stopped in the middle of the road while you stare at your phone.

What surprised me was the anxiety that came with it. Not the bold, clarifying anxiety of doing something hard — facing the dragon, attempting something that might fail. That anxiety has a direction to it. This was different: quieter, more corrosive. A background hum. Running in circles. Knowing you should be somewhere else but not being ready to say it out loud. Unable to affect change, hitting a ceiling with no promotion in sight, mastering your craft in a room that had stopped growing — treading water, which it turns out has its own particular kind of dread. That feeling, I've learned, is the bus telling you it's stopped.

The real danger isn't comfort. It's mistaking comfort for arrival.

The easy bus comes. Just make sure it's still going somewhere.

---

*Mark Schenker is a teacher and writer based in Singapore.*
