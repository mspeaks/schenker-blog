In February 2018, I walked into a small room on the ninth floor of a building in City Hall, Singapore, and taught my first ever solo adult English class.

I was terrified.

I had taught English before — to kids in Japan, always with a co-teacher beside me to share the load, to catch me when I stumbled. This was different. These were adults. People who had paid their own money to be in that room. And I was the only one standing at the front of it.

I remember obsessing over every lesson plan. The progression of activities. The timing. The transition from warm-up to presentation to practice. I wanted every minute to count because I knew these students were trusting me with theirs.

What I didn't expect was how much the school itself would show up for me.

![A ULC classroom lesson, January 2020](/images/ulc-classroom-2020.jpg)

---

Matt, the founder of ULC, did something I've never forgotten. Before I was even officially hired, he paid me in cash to cover lessons. That's how much he believed in building something real. He wasn't just filling slots on a schedule — he was investing in people. He took me under his wing in a way that went well beyond job training. He made me feel, from day one, like I was already part of something.

The early team felt the same way. Marialena. Norah. Nat. Teachers who were already exceptional at what they did and generous enough to share it. I was inspired watching them work. I wanted to be as good as they were. That competitive admiration — the healthy kind — pushed me to put in the hours.

And the hours were real. In those early years, over 120 teaching hours a month wasn't unusual. It was the culture. And honestly? I was grateful for it. There's no substitute for repetition when you're learning a craft. You teach the same grammar structure twelve different ways until you find the one that makes a student's eyes light up. You figure out which activities fall flat and which ones have a room buzzing. You build a sixth sense for when a lesson is working and when it's quietly dying.

By the end of my first year, I had found my footing. By the end of my second, I had found my voice.

---

Growth at ULC was steady and real. We expanded from that ninth-floor room to the tenth. We built programs, curriculum, a culture.

2021 was a year I'll remember for a long time. Despite everything the world threw at everyone, ULC thrived. I received a two-month bonus — a sure windfall compared to anything I had experienced in my previous jobs. It felt like proof that the work mattered an my career was moving in the right direction.

And then COVID hit properly, and we adapted.

Matt went out and bought iPads. We connected them to GoodNotes and ran Zoom classes with a live digital whiteboard — handwritten notes, diagrams, vocabulary lists, all visible in real time. Students who couldn't go home stayed in Singapore and kept studying. The school kept running. A lot of businesses didn't survive that period. We did.

During those years, Matt and I also took ULC's approach to learning online in a different way — we built a YouTube channel together, [Teachers Mark and Matt](https://www.youtube.com/c/teachersmarkandmatt), producing hundreds of English learning videos from 2020 to 2023. It was one of the most creatively satisfying projects of my time at ULC, and a reminder that good teaching can reach people far beyond the classroom.

---

2023 brought the biggest leap of faith: a second branch. Somerset 111. New energy, new ambitions, a genuine belief that what we'd built could grow. As with any expansion, things didn't unfold exactly as planned, and the business eventually returned to a single centre at City Hall — the original, the one we knew, the one we'd built.

2025 has been a regrowth story. Rebuilding the student community. Rebuilding the culture. Reminding ourselves — and our students — what made this school worth coming back to.

---

But if I'm honest about what I'm most proud of, it's not the hours or the curriculum or the YouTube views.

It's the students.

One of my former students, Sehee Park, left a review that I keep coming back to. She wrote:

> *"This place is not just for studying English. The thing I like the most is I am not afraid of speaking in English anymore. Because they gave me plenty of opportunities to connect with people from different nationalities. I made a lot of friends here and this is the most important thing for living in a different country as a foreigner."*

That's it. That's what we do at our best.

Think about who walks through ULC's doors. A woman who has moved to Singapore with her husband for his work — new country, no friends, no professional network, no safety net. A mother from China who uprooted her life so her children could get a better education, now navigating a foreign city largely on her own. These students come to us not just to improve their grammar. They come to us to find their footing in a new life.

We teach English. But what we really do is help people become part of a community. Help them feel less alone. Help them build the confidence to not just survive in Singapore but to actually enjoy it.

Sehee is one of those students. So is Kayla — a student who stayed at upper-intermediate level for years, not because her English wasn't improving, but because ULC had become her support system. Her people. Her place. The English was almost beside the point.

Those are the students I'm most proud of. And over 14 or 15 years, ULC has helped thousands of them. Thousands of people who came to Singapore as strangers and left with friends, confidence, and a community they built one lesson at a time.

---

I leave ULC having taught over 8,000 hours of adult English. Three editions of *English File*. Hundreds of students. A career that grew from nervous new teacher to social media manager to academic manager — every role a new way of contributing to the same school, the same mission.

Most of all, I've been part of a community. The kind that celebrates when students succeed, that supports each other on the difficult days, that has a warmth to it that most workplaces only claim to have.

Eight years. Roughly 20% of my life. And every hour of it was worth it.

Thank you, ULC. What a privilege to have been part of your story.

---

*Mark Schenker · ULC, 2018–2026*
